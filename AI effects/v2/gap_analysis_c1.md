## New themes flagged by agents

*These are themes not covered by the current claim taxonomy — candidates for Cycle 2 expansion or a new claims section.*

- **[jakesch2023cowriting]** Latent persuasion by language models — a distinct mechanism whereby AI writing assistants subtly shift opinions of users through suggestion architecture, not just through generated content viewed passively
- **[tredinnick2025epistemic]** Model collapse / ouroboric training loop — generative models trained on synthetic outputs of prior models leading to sudden collapse of generative capacity, a distinct technical/epistemic risk beyond gradual quality decline
- **[ferrara2024genai]** Nefarious uses of GenAI for impersonation, fraud, deepfakes, and synthetic identities — a broader information-ecosystem harm framework beyond the white paper's current scope (e.g., AI-driven scams, voice cloning, fabricated evidence)
- **[savoldi2025practices]** Gender divides in AI adoption and usage — women systematically underrepresented as adopters and users, representing a distinct dimension of inequality not currently captured in the EC-9 framing
- **[friedmann2025great]** Shadow banning / algorithmic de-ranking on social media platforms as a parallel mechanism to generative search for reducing content visibility — distinct from AI-related claims but part of the same broader pattern of platform power over information access
- **[friedmann2025great]** Legal/regulatory remedies for generative search harms (copyright, unfair competition, unjust enrichment, China's RAR, EU AI Act) — not a claim direction but a policy-response dimension the white paper may want to cover
- **[carson2023symbiosis]** News Media Bargaining Code / regulatory countermeasures as policy lever for publisher revenue
- **[carson2023symbiosis]** Platform dependency as antecedent of AI-era traffic decline
- **[strauss2025attribution]** Standardized telemetry/OpenTelemetry as policy intervention for attribution accountability
- **[strauss2025attribution]** Citation efficiency as a measurable and tractable design choice
- **[lundstrmimanov2026economics]** Model collapse as economic externality requiring Pigouvian subsidy / watermarking
- **[lundstrmimanov2026economics]** Provenance certification and human-data subsidies as policy levers
- **[lundstrmimanov2026economics]** Endogenous data-quality deterioration as new microeconomic concept
- **[hernndez2025towards]** Global South / pluriversal AI governance
- **[hernndez2025towards]** Environmental & water/energy externalities of AI infrastructure
- **[hernndez2025towards]** Data-worker exploitation in AI supply chain
- **[baack2025towards]** Decline of the open web due to mass opt-outs and AI crawler blocking
- **[baack2025towards]** Need for reciprocity / compensation frameworks for data contributors (e.g., Wikimedia Enterprise model)
- **[borji2023categorical]** Plagiarism / academic integrity risks from ChatGPT
- **[borji2023categorical]** AI political/ideological bias in chatbot outputs
- **[spinellis2025false]** AI-generated content in predatory journals contaminating scholarly indices (Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, Crossref DOI system) — identity verification gaps in ORCID/DOI infrastructure
- **[dada2026usage]** Global South / sub-Saharan Africa-specific information literacy gap for AI tools — infrastructure constraints (electricity, data costs) interacting with critical evaluation skill development
- **[bareither2026hybrid]** 'Epistemic messiness' — concept describing simultaneous strengthening and weakening of epistemic relations through hybrid human-AI practices
- **[bareither2026hybrid]** Mistrust and antagonism between educators and students arising from unreliable AI-detection tools
- **[bareither2026hybrid]** Bottom-up 'ethics-in-practice' emerging where institutional guidance is absent
- **[kuznetsova2023trust]** language inequity in LLM veracity assessment; low-resource languages disproportionately receive non-responses or worse accuracy on disinformation tasks
- **[macarthur2025language]** rhetorical debt as analog of technical debt: accumulating costs of unreviewed AI-generated writing in professional knowledge work
- **[denina2026wikipedia]** Community health measurement infrastructure: the paper introduces a live, auto-updating dashboard system (Community Vital Signs) for tracking editor retention, stability, generational balance, and role renewal across 358+ Wikipedia language editions — providing empirical tooling to detect pipeline erosion trends, but not itself documenting an AI-caused decline
- **[hwang2022rules]** Governance divergence across language editions: the paper documents that Wikipedia language communities, while sharing goals and infrastructure, develop increasingly distinct rule sets over time — a structural governance finding that contextualizes how AI-era pressures might affect communities differently depending on their governance maturity
- **[warnckewang2023increasing]** Newcomer retention as a structural challenge predating AI: the paper demonstrates through a large-scale RCT (244,060 accounts, 27 non-English Wikipedias) that interventions to improve newcomer activation and retention produce only modest effects — suggesting the editor pipeline problem is structurally deep and not easily reversed, making AI-era erosion harder to counteract
- **[warnckewang2023increasing]** Diversity and inclusion as pipeline factors: barriers to entry disproportionately affect women and underrepresented groups; interventions like the Newcomer Homepage are expected to help these groups more, linking pipeline health to community diversity
- **[schwitter2024offline]** Offline-to-online community resilience: empirical evidence that face-to-face meetups measurably slow editor attrition in Wikipedia, suggesting community-building interventions as a lever against contributor decline — not captured by existing taxonomy themes.
- **[klempert2025wikipedias]** Digital sovereignty of open knowledge infrastructure: the paper frames Wikipedia's independence — from for-profit actors, state censorship, and large funders — as an ongoing organizational challenge; this 'knowledge commons sovereignty' dimension is not directly represented in the taxonomy.
- **[klempert2025wikipedias]** Structural dependency of Wikimedia's funding model on web traffic: the small-donor fundraising model's explicit dependence on website visits creates a structural vulnerability to AI-driven traffic displacement — this mechanism is more specific than D1's general framing.
- **[brown2025webscraping]** Research data access crisis caused by AI-driven platform lockdown: the paper documents how AI companies' demand for scraped training data has prompted platforms to drastically restrict or monetize API access, directly harming academic researchers — a collateral harm to the research commons not captured by the existing E or F themes.
- **[brown2025webscraping]** Legal ambiguity of scraping as a systemic infrastructure issue: the paper frames the unsettled legal landscape around scraping (CFAA, ToS, GDPR) as itself a form of governance failure that increases costs and uncertainty for legitimate public-interest data access.
- **[kretschmer2024copyright]** Legal gray zone enabling 'data extractivism': the paper identifies a structural dynamic where legal uncertainty incentivises AI firms to aggressively scrape and then destroy training data to avoid liability — a form of legally-enabled value extraction with no compensation mechanism currently in place
- **[kretschmer2024copyright]** API design as a covert TDM restriction mechanism: platform operators use API throttling and premium tiers to circumvent mandatory TDM rights, creating techno-legal loopholes that undermine open-access research
- **[oecd2025intellectual]** Data provenance opacity as a systemic governance problem: the Stanford CRFM finding that only 1 of 14 leading AI developers discloses training data origins, and that transparency dropped from 20% to 7% between 2023 and 2024, represents a distinct and measurable trend not fully captured in the existing taxonomy
- **[oecd2025intellectual]** Jurisdictional fragmentation as a barrier to enforcement: cross-border scraping operations exploit regulatory gaps between jurisdictions, creating an enforcement vacuum that existing claim themes do not explicitly address
- **[hanley2024machinemade]** AI-enabled misinformation industrialization: the 474% increase in synthetic articles on misinformation sites post-ChatGPT documents a qualitatively new phenomenon — the large-scale, low-cost industrialization of AI-generated disinformation on fringe/unreliable news sites — which goes beyond the existing G1 theme (which is Wikipedia-focused) and H1 (which is about hallucination quality) to describe a structural shift in the misinformation supply chain
- **[hanley2024machinemade]** Size-stratified AI adoption: the finding that synthetic content growth is concentrated in smaller, less popular websites (with the largest sites showing little change) suggests a digital divide in AI content adoption that intersects with platform reach and credibility
- **[liu2024somesite]** Data licensing deals as an emerging market mechanism: some publishers removed robots.txt blocks after entering paid data licensing agreements with AI companies (OpenAI deals with Stack Exchange, Conde Nast, Dotdash Meredith), suggesting a nascent licensing market that could partially address EC-1 but raises questions about who benefits and on what terms.
- **[liu2024somesite]** Asymmetric protection capacity: individual creators (artists) are far less able to deploy countermeasures than large institutional rightsholders, creating a structural equity gap in content protection.
- **[boxem2026guys]** Identity spoofing arms race: a large share of bots deliberately spoof browser user-agent strings to evade detection (our method flags 491 unique user agents versus Cloudflare's 58), suggesting that the scale of covert automated scraping is substantially larger than commonly assumed and that published traffic figures likely undercount bot load.
- **[boxem2026guys]** Wikimedia as an explicit case study of bot-driven infrastructure strain: the paper directly cites Wikimedia's April 2025 and 2026 blog posts as illustrative evidence, making it a strong source for B1/E1/E2 in the Wikipedia-specific context.
- **[peukert2025copyright]** Regulatory framework as partial remedy for EC-1/E-cluster harms: the EU GPAI Code of Practice establishes an opt-out compliance mechanism (robots.txt + future machine-readable protocols) that, if followed, would give rightsholders legal standing and a practical tool to withhold consent for training-data use — a structural intervention not captured in the current taxonomy.
- **[peukert2025copyright]** Retroactive compliance problem: the Code requires providers to apply copyright policy 'throughout the entire lifecycle' of models, including retroactively to training data already collected before market entry, creating a novel legal uncertainty for existing deployed models.
- **[peukert2025copyright]** Global regulatory spillover: 26 major GPAIM providers (Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, etc.) are signatories, and the Code claims extraterritorial effect for any model placed on the EU market regardless of where training occurred — potentially globalizing the EC-1 remedy regime.
- **[zhong2025intellectual]** Technical countermeasures (semantic HTML policy injection) as a new class of defense against LLM content extraction — distinct from robots.txt/CAPTCHAs and worth noting as an emerging mitigation strategy
